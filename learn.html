🔬 How a Tadpole Becomes a Frog

This process is called metamorphosis — one of the most amazing things in all of nature. Here's what happens step by step:

🥚

Eggs

Frogs lay clusters of tiny eggs in still water. Each egg has a clear jelly coating.

🐟

Tadpole — No Legs

After 1–3 weeks, eggs hatch into tadpoles. They breathe through gills and eat algae.

🦵

Back Legs Appear

Around 5–9 weeks, tiny back legs bud out near the tail. You can see little toes forming!

🤲

Front Legs Appear

Front legs push through next. The tadpole starts breathing air and needs to reach the surface.

🐸

Froglet

The tail shrinks as the body absorbs it. The froglet can now hop on land but still has a small tail.

🌿

Young Frog

Tail fully gone! A fully formed frog, ready to explore the world beyond the pond.

⭐ Fun Facts

Frogs don't drink water. They absorb it through their skin! That's why clean water is so important to them.

A tadpole's tail disappears completely because the body absorbs it as a source of nutrition during the transformation. Nothing is wasted!

Frogs are indicator species. Scientists watch frog populations to understand the health of an entire ecosystem.

Maryland has 19 species of frogs and toads. Common ones near the Chesapeake Bay area include the Green Frog and American Bullfrog.

Water temperature matters. Warmer water speeds up development. That's why we track it in every entry!

Frogs breathe two ways — through their lungs AND through their moist skin. They can even absorb oxygen underwater this way.

🌊 About Our Pond

We're watching tadpoles grow at Bay Innovation Farm in Stevensville, Maryland on the Eastern Shore of the Chesapeake Bay.

Our pond, The Learning Pond, is a small freshwater habitat we've set aside to encourage local wildlife. Frogs are a sign of a healthy ecosystem — and we're thrilled they chose our farm to call home!
